MINESWEEPER

The Code


Let's set the scene.

The date is August 2nd, 2018, and I am sitting on a plane on my way to Boston to see my friend for the weekend. Attempting to quell my boredom, I begin playing Minesweeper on my phone, a common thing to do when I don't have internet. However, today was no ordinary day of playing my favorite arcade game. I had long since known about the pitfalls of Minesweeper - times when the player has no choice but to guess what cell is a mine and what isn't. I hate these moments; in a game solely about logic, it infuriates me when I lose due to circumstances outside of my control. With a 4 hour layover in Philadelphia, I had a plan. A plan that would make the greatest designers tremble. I would attempt to fix a decades-old game, beloved by fans everywhere.

I would attempt to fix Minesweeper

The Goal

Minesweeper is a fantastic game that most everyone from the 1960s-onwards should know. The game, which began as a mainframe game in the 1960s, has a very simple premise: clear out a minefield by clicking on cells. Almost every cell has a number, and that number tells the player how many mines are adjacent.

Impossible Move

It's a very simple logic game, but it has one glaring flaw that upsets me. Due to the random nature of the game, it is possible (and quite likely) to get into a situation where you have no choice but to guess as to which cell is a mine and which one isn't. This can be seen in the screenshot to the right. In this shot, it is clear that the top two cells are mines, but it is unclear whether the middle cell is a mine or if the bottom cell is a mine. This is by far not the only occurrence of this conundrum, but it seems to be the most common.

The goal of this project is deceivingly simple: fix Minesweeper's unfair guessing game. However, like most things, it is easier said than done.

The Future

As stated earlier, this project is not done. The basic functionality of the game is there, but my goal was not to simply recreate Minesweeper. The goal was to fix it.
Unfortunately, I don't really know how to accomplish this task currently, nor do I really have the time to do it. Some plans I currently have include creating a bot that will solve the puzzle first and figure out what is impossible and what isn't. After that, I can think of a way to resolve these situations. During these situations, I was thinking another, smaller map would pop up. If the player completed this map, the situation would automatically be resolved, and the correct mine would be flagged. There is also the option of having a special tool that players can use to flag impossible situations. At the end of the game, the game would check these so-called "impossible situations". If they truly can't be resolved logically, the player would win. If not, the mines would explode and the player would lose. These ideas are admittedly not great, and need some refining. However, I hope that once I get the time to finish this project, implementing these ideas won't be too difficult.
