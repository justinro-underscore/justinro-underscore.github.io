TERMINAL

The Code


Ah, hackathons/codeathons. If you've never experienced a hackathon before, here's how it's supposed to work: you get a small team together, think of a small, manageable project, and start working on it! You sleep for about 6 hours Friday night, maybe 5 hours Saturday night, and have good, filling meals at normal times. You spend your time socializing with other programmers, talking to potential employers, and spend the rest of the time coding while taking frequent breaks to keep stress levels down.

At least, that's how it's supposed to work.
Welcome to Terminal, the ambitious product of our hackathon experience.

The Game

Terminal is an asychronous, cooperative, puzzle, couch-multiplayer game with some unique mechanics. Player One is in charge of the top two-thirds of the screen, and for them the game is a platformer. They use a controller and are able to jump around the environment, utilizing special abilities like wall jumps and a high jump. Player Two is in charge of the lower third of the screen, and for them the game is a combination of a typing and puzzle game. Player Two uses the keyboard, and takes on the role of a hacker, typing in commands to a terminal. The "hacker" (Player Two) is able to manipulate the "spy's" (Player One's) environment. The two players must work together in order to complete levels by using a combination of skillful platforming, clever puzzle solving, and fast typing skills.

The Teamwork

The team that worked on this project was fairly small. Alex Shadley worked on the code along with me, Harry Luo helped a bit on the code and designed level two, Jesse Truong worked on the sprites, and my roommate Tim Larson wrote the music (he also wrote the music for another one of my projects, Cannoneer). I functioned as a psuedo-design lead, while also working heavily on the codebase.

The Experience

Jayhacks Logo

This game was created in 36 hours at the Jayhacks 2017 hackathon (now HackKU), a hackathon that I would end up being on the board for the next year! The experience was exciting, as I was awake for 31 of the 36 hours spent coding. This really taught me how to handle my emotions, as when you're awake for more than 24 hours straight working on things that should work, you're likely to snap at your teammates. Thankfully, everytime my teammates and I would get into an argument, we would take a step back, clear our heads, then work together to get to a solution. We knew that arguments lead to nothing, and only when we worked together could we solve our problems. This proved to be a great skill, as the team that stayed in the same room as us was unable to resolve their conflicts and came out with a subpar product.

The Reception

When the coding was over and we were all smelly and tired, we immediately had to go and present our projects. Presentations were done in a large, open area, "science fair"-style. We set up our project on a table, and the judges and other contestants would walk around to see what we accomplished. When they came over, we gave them a brief overview of the game and let them play. Watching two people play and enjoy the game that I and my teammates had worked so hard on in the past few hours was exhilirating. Seeing them laugh and cheer when they figured out one of the more complex puzzles in our game gave me so much joy that I didn't stop smiling the entire time. This was definitely one of the best moments of my life.
Oh, and we ended up winning as well! Terminal won second place at the awards ceremony, and we all ended up receiving free Beats headphones! It was a great time, and we were all very proud of the work we accomplished.
