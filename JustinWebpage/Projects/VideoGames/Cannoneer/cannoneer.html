CANNONEER

The Code


In the spring of 2018, I took a class on game design. I learned a lot game theory and critical analysis of games, but my favorite part of the class was the final project, where we had to create our own game. Throughout the semester we had to submit Design Proposals (which were basically just simplified GDDs), and our final project had to be based on one of these proposals. At the end of the semester, when it came time to finally develop our project, I knew exactly what I wanted to make. It was an homage to the arcade games I grew up playing, the ones that defined the way games were played for generations. It combined old-school design mechanics with a sea-worthy theme. I decided to make Cannoneer.

"Ay, so ye landlubbers think ye has what it takes to be a pirate captain? ‘Tis a dangerous life, and one must be quick with a wheel and a cannon if ye wishes to claim the booty. However, if ye heart is set and ye resolve is steely, give it a whirl. However, don’t be surprised when we meet in Davy Jones’ Locker..."

~Text Written on the Cabinet


The Mechanics

Gameplay

Cannoneer is a horizontal shooter with vertical shooter mechanics, made with Unity. In the game, you take control of a pirate ship, and must evade and destroy enemy ships that appear above and below you, using your port and starboard side cannons. The game takes on a horizontal shooter feel, as the game moves from left to right and enemies come from the right side of the screen as opposed to the left, but all of the shooting occurs on the vertical y-axis. Shooting has a cool-down, simulating a cannon reloading, which means players have think twice about the shots they take. The game is an homage to classic 1980s arcade games, and thus, is best played in a cabinet. Going with the arcade feel, the goal of the game is to survive infinite waves of enemies while trying to get the high score. Each progressive level gets more difficult, and different enemies appear the farther into the game you get. There are also secrets in the game, if you can find them...

The Teamwork

Credits for the Game

While I did a majority of the work on the project, I was not the only person working on it. For one, I had my partner on the project, Luke Johnson, who worked a majority on the art, documentation, and exhibition of the project. I also had help from my roommate Tim Larson, who made the soundtracks for the game, as well as my friend Sean Poole, who made the original ship and rock sprites for the game. I was in charge of the programming as well as the mechanics of the game. I was also in charge of wiring up the hardware and getting the cabinet controls working.

The Exhibition

No great arcade game is complete without an arcade cabinet to hold it. There is something special about playing a game with a standard joystick and buttons - a feeling that harkens back to a simpler time of video games. A time that I was unable to witness, but one that I longed to recreate. The cabinet was simple, just a wooden structure that housed a monitor and speakers. This monitor was then hooked up to a laptop that had an encoder board with the joystick and buttons hooked into it.

The Reception

The project was a huge success. Luke did a fantastic job on the painting of the cabinet, and Tim's soundtrack combined with Luke and Seans' sprites really gave the game that old, arcade feeling that we were shooting for (no pun intended). When programming, I tried to stay away from tutorials and learn things for myself which, although very difficult at times, really immersed me in how Unity works and gave me a good idea on how to work on games using Unity in the future.
Oh, and the game actually won an award! At KU Film & Media Studies's Tensies Awards, Cannoneer came away with winning the Best New Media award, which was a huge honor!
